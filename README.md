## What are the factors affecting consumer ?

Consumer behavior is influenced by various factors, which can be broadly categorized into four main groups: Psychological, Personal, Social, and Cultural factors. Each plays a crucial role in shaping a consumer‚Äôs purchasing decisions. Let‚Äôs break them down:

1. Psychological Factors (Internal Influences)

	‚Ä¢	Motivation: Driven by Maslow‚Äôs Hierarchy of Needs‚Äîbasic (physiological) to self-actualization needs.
	‚Ä¢	Learning: Past experiences, habits, and exposure influence choices.
	‚Ä¢	Attitudes & Beliefs: A consumer‚Äôs mindset about a product or brand, shaped by prior interactions.

2. Personal Factors (Individual Characteristics)

These are unique to each consumer and influence buying behavior.
	‚Ä¢	Age & Life Stage: Needs change as consumers grow older (e.g., a student vs. a retired person).
	‚Ä¢	Income & Economic Status: Affects purchasing power and product preferences.
	‚Ä¢	Occupation: Determines needs (e.g., a corporate executive may prefer premium brands).
	‚Ä¢	Lifestyle & Personality: Different people have different spending habits based on their values and interests.

	‚Ä¢	Family: Parents, spouses, and children affect purchasing decisions.
	‚Ä¢	Reference Groups: Friends, colleagues, or celebrities can influence choices.
	‚Ä¢	Social Status & Roles: Higher social class consumers may prefer luxury goods.
	‚Ä¢	Opinion Leaders & Influencers: Social media personalities, bloggers, or industry experts shape trends.

4. Cultural Factors (Societal Influences)

Culture plays a fundamental role in shaping consumer behavior.
	‚Ä¢	Culture & Subculture: Traditions, beliefs, and values impact preferences.
	‚Ä¢	Social Class: Influences spending patterns (upper, middle, or lower class).
	‚Ä¢	Regional & Ethnic Influences: People from different regions may have unique purchasing preferences.

Other Factors Affecting Consumers:

Apart from these core influences, some situational and environmental factors also play a role:
	‚Ä¢	Marketing Mix (4Ps): Product, Price, Place, Promotion impact choices.
	‚Ä¢	Technology & Digital Trends: Online reviews, e-commerce, and social media shape preferences.
	‚Ä¢	Economic Conditions: Inflation, recession, or boom affects consumer spending.


## Stages in consumer decision making process ??

Stages in the Consumer Decision-Making Process üõçÔ∏è

The consumer decision-making process is a structured journey that a consumer follows before purchasing a product or service. It consists of five key stages:

1. Need Recognition (Problem Identification)
	‚Ä¢	The process starts when the consumer realizes a need or problem.
	‚Ä¢	This need can be triggered by internal factors (hunger, thirst, fashion trends) or external factors (advertisements, peer influence).
	‚Ä¢	Example: You realize your old phone is slow and needs replacement.

2. Information Search
	‚Ä¢	The consumer looks for solutions and information about available options.
	‚Ä¢	Sources of information:
	‚Ä¢	Internal Search: Past experiences, memory.
	‚Ä¢	External Search: Friends, online reviews, advertisements, influencers.
	‚Ä¢	Example: You browse online reviews, visit stores, and watch YouTube comparisons.

3. Evaluation of Alternatives
	‚Ä¢	Consumers compare different brands/products based on features, price, quality, and benefits.
	‚Ä¢	They may use:
	‚Ä¢	Compensatory Decision Rule ‚Äì trade-offs (e.g., higher price but better quality).
	‚Ä¢	Non-Compensatory Rule ‚Äì strict criteria (e.g., only considering a brand with 5G).
	‚Ä¢	Example: You compare an iPhone vs. Samsung based on price, camera, battery, and brand loyalty.

4. Purchase Decision
	‚Ä¢	The consumer makes the final choice and proceeds with the purchase.
	‚Ä¢	Influencing factors:
	‚Ä¢	Brand loyalty
	‚Ä¢	Discounts & promotions
	‚Ä¢	Salesperson influence
	‚Ä¢	Example: You decide to buy an iPhone from Amazon because of a limited-time discount.

5. Post-Purchase Behavior
	‚Ä¢	After purchase, the consumer evaluates satisfaction or dissatisfaction.
	‚Ä¢	Outcomes:
	‚Ä¢	Satisfied ‚Üí Positive reviews, repeat purchase, brand loyalty.
	‚Ä¢	Dissatisfied ‚Üí Complaints, product returns, negative reviews.
	‚Ä¢	Example: If the iPhone meets expectations, you recommend it to friends. If not, you leave a bad review.

Bonus: Additional Stages

Some models add:
	‚Ä¢	Zero Moment of Truth (ZMOT) ‚Äì The influence of online searches before the buying journey.
	‚Ä¢	Post-Purchase Engagement ‚Äì Companies engage customers post-sale through support and loyalty programs.

In Consumer Behavior, memory plays a crucial role in how consumers perceive, retain, and recall information about brands, products, and experiences. Memory can be categorized into different types:

1. Sensory Memory

üîπ Definition: Stores information from senses (sight, sound, smell, taste, touch) for a very short duration (a few milliseconds to a few seconds).
üîπ Role in Consumer Behavior:
	‚Ä¢	Influences first impressions of a product or advertisement.

	‚Ä¢	Consumers use it while browsing online, shopping in a store, or comparing options.
	‚Ä¢	If the information is repeated or engaging, it may move to long-term memory.

	‚Ä¢	Brand Recall: Ability to remember a brand name when thinking about a product category (e.g., ‚ÄúWhat soda do you know?‚Äù ‚Üí Coke, Pepsi).
	‚Ä¢	Brand Recognition: Ability to recognize a brand when seen/heard (e.g., recognizing McDonald‚Äôs golden arches).

How Marketers Use Memory in Consumer Behavior?

‚úî Repetition ‚Äì Frequent ads help move brand information to long-term memory.
‚úî Emotional Triggers ‚Äì Nostalgia, humor, or storytelling enhances recall.
‚úî Brand Association ‚Äì Linking brands with colors, slogans, and jingles (e.g., ‚ÄúJust Do It‚Äù ‚Üí Nike).

## Segmentation, targetting and positioning 

Segmentation, Targeting, and Positioning (STP) Framework

The STP model is a fundamental marketing strategy that helps businesses identify the right consumers, tailor their messaging, and position their products effectively. It consists of three key steps:

1. Segmentation ‚Äì Dividing the Market

Definition:
Market segmentation involves dividing a broad market into smaller, manageable groups based on shared characteristics. The goal is to identify consumer groups with similar needs, behaviors, or preferences.

Types of Market Segmentation
	1.	Demographic Segmentation ‚Äì Based on age, gender, income, education, occupation, family status.
	‚Ä¢	Example: Luxury brands target high-income groups; baby products target young parents.
	2.	Geographic Segmentation ‚Äì Based on location (country, city, climate, urban/rural).
	‚Ä¢	Example: Winter jackets sell better in colder regions than in tropical areas.
	3.	Psychographic Segmentation ‚Äì Based on lifestyle, values, personality, and interests.
	‚Ä¢	Example: Eco-conscious consumers prefer sustainable brands.
	4.	Behavioral Segmentation ‚Äì Based on purchasing behavior, brand loyalty, product usage.
	‚Ä¢	Example: Airline companies offer frequent flyer programs for loyal customers.

Why it matters?
	‚Ä¢	Helps brands understand their customers better
	‚Ä¢	Allows for personalized marketing strategies
	‚Ä¢	Improves efficiency in resource allocation and advertising

2. Targeting ‚Äì Selecting the Right Segment

Definition:
After segmenting the market, businesses evaluate and choose the most profitable and suitable segment(s) to focus on.

Types of Targeting Strategies
	1.	Undifferentiated (Mass Marketing) ‚Äì One-size-fits-all approach.
	‚Ä¢	Example: Coca-Cola markets its drink to everyone, not just a specific segment.
	2.	Differentiated (Segmented Marketing) ‚Äì Different strategies for different segments.
	‚Ä¢	Example: Nike markets running shoes to athletes and casual sneakers to youth.
	3.	Concentrated (Niche Marketing) ‚Äì Focus on a single segment with specialized needs.
	‚Ä¢	Example: Rolex targets high-income luxury buyers.
	4.	Micromarketing (One-to-One Marketing) ‚Äì Hyper-personalized marketing for individuals.
	‚Ä¢	Example: Amazon recommends products based on past purchases.

How do companies choose a target market?
	‚Ä¢	Market attractiveness (size, growth potential, profitability).
	‚Ä¢	Competitive advantage (can the company compete effectively in this segment?).
	‚Ä¢	Brand alignment (does the segment fit the company‚Äôs vision?).

3. Positioning ‚Äì Creating a Unique Brand Image

Definition:
Positioning is about creating a distinct image of a product in the consumer‚Äôs mind compared to competitors.

Steps in Positioning
	1.	Identify key attributes that consumers value.
	2.	Analyze competitor positioning.
	3.	Develop a Unique Selling Proposition (USP).
	4.	Communicate the positioning through branding, messaging, and marketing efforts.

Types of Positioning Strategies
	1.	Product-Based Positioning ‚Äì Focus on quality, features, or innovation.
	‚Ä¢	Example: Apple markets the iPhone as a premium, innovative device.
	2.	Price-Based Positioning ‚Äì Competing on affordability or exclusivity.
	‚Ä¢	Example: Walmart positions itself as a low-cost retailer, while Rolex uses premium pricing.
	3.	Benefit-Based Positioning ‚Äì Highlighting functional or emotional benefits.
	‚Ä¢	Example: Colgate promotes stronger teeth & cavity protection.
	4.	Use/Application-Based Positioning ‚Äì Positioning based on product use.
	‚Ä¢	Example: Gatorade is positioned as a sports recovery drink.

Positioning Map (Perceptual Map)

A positioning map visually shows how consumers perceive different brands based on attributes like price, quality, or features.

üöÄ Example:
If you compare Tesla vs. Toyota:
	‚Ä¢	Tesla = High Price + High Innovation
	‚Ä¢	Toyota = Affordable + Reliable

Example: STP in Action ‚Äì Starbucks ‚òï
	‚Ä¢	Segmentation:
	‚Ä¢	Demographic: Middle-to-upper-income professionals.
	‚Ä¢	Psychographic: People who value premium coffee and experiences.
	‚Ä¢	Targeting:
	‚Ä¢	Targets young professionals, students, and coffee enthusiasts who prefer a premium caf√© experience.

## Harvard Sheet model

Harvard Sheet Model of Consumer Behavior

The Harvard Sheet Model is an analytical framework used to understand consumer decision-making and behavior by examining key internal and external factors that influence purchasing choices. This model is commonly used in MBA marketing courses and consumer behavior studies.

üîπ Components of the Harvard Sheet Model

The model consists of the following five key elements that affect consumer behavior:
	1.	Input Variables (External Influences)
	2.	Process Variables (Internal Psychological Factors)
	3.	Output Variables (Consumer Response & Decision-Making)
	4.	Feedback Loop (Post-Purchase Evaluation)
	5.	Situational Factors (Environmental & Market Context)

1Ô∏è‚É£ Input Variables (External Influences)

These are external factors that influence consumer decisions before they even start the buying process.

Key Influences:
‚úî Marketing Stimuli (4Ps): Product, Price, Place, Promotion.
‚úî Environmental Factors: Culture, social class, family, reference groups.
‚úî Economic Conditions: Consumer income, inflation, economic stability.
‚úî Technological Advances: Digital advertising, AI-driven recommendations.

Example: A consumer sees an advertisement for Nike shoes, a reference group (influencers) endorses it, and a discount offer triggers interest.

2Ô∏è‚É£ Process Variables (Internal Psychological Factors)

These are the internal cognitive processes that shape how a consumer thinks, feels, and decides on a purchase.

Key Psychological Factors:
‚úî Motivation (Maslow‚Äôs Hierarchy of Needs) ‚Äì What drives the purchase?
‚úî Perception ‚Äì How does the consumer interpret the brand‚Äôs message?
‚úî Learning & Memory ‚Äì Past experiences with the brand.
‚úî Attitude Formation ‚Äì Brand loyalty, trust, and emotions toward a product.

Example: A consumer who trusts Nike‚Äôs brand quality (positive past experience) is more likely to buy their products again.

3Ô∏è‚É£ Output Variables (Consumer Response & Decision-Making)

This stage involves actual consumer behavior and decisions based on the earlier influences.

Stages of Decision-Making (Similar to the Consumer Decision Process Model):
	1.	Problem Recognition ‚Äì Identifying a need.
	2.	Information Search ‚Äì Researching options.
	3.	Evaluation of Alternatives ‚Äì Comparing brands.
	4.	Purchase Decision ‚Äì Making the purchase.
	5.	Post-Purchase Behavior ‚Äì Satisfaction, loyalty, or dissatisfaction.

Example: After comparing Nike vs. Adidas, the consumer chooses Nike based on quality, price, and brand perception.

4Ô∏è‚É£ Feedback Loop (Post-Purchase Evaluation)

After purchasing, the consumer evaluates whether the product meets expectations or not. This influences future behavior.

Possible Outcomes:
‚úî Positive Feedback: Satisfaction ‚Üí Repeat Purchase & Brand Loyalty.
‚úî Negative Feedback: Dissatisfaction ‚Üí Complaints, Negative Reviews, Switching Brands.

Example: If the Nike shoes are comfortable and durable, the consumer recommends them to friends. If they wear out too fast, the consumer switches to Adidas next time.

5Ô∏è‚É£ Situational Factors (Environmental & Market Context)

External, temporary conditions that can impact consumer choices.

Examples of Situational Factors:
‚úî Seasonality: Buying winter jackets in December vs. swimsuits in summer.
‚úî Point of Sale Influence: Impulse purchases due to in-store promotions.
‚úî Economic Conditions: Recession ‚Üí More budget-friendly purchases.

Example: A consumer may buy Nike shoes during a Black Friday sale due to limited-time discounts.

üí° Key Takeaways of the Harvard Sheet Model

‚úî Combines external and internal factors for a complete understanding of consumer behavior.
‚úî Helps marketers create targeted strategies by analyzing key influences.
‚úî Explains why consumers make specific choices based on psychology, environment, and decision-making processes.


## Nicosia model

Nicosia Model of Consumer Behavior

The Nicosia Model, developed by Francesco Nicosia, is one of the earliest models of consumer decision-making. It focuses on the relationship between the company and the consumer and explains how advertising and communication influence consumer behavior in a step-by-step process.

üîπ Key Assumptions of the Nicosia Model

‚úî The model emphasizes the interaction between the company and the consumer before making a purchase decision.

‚úî It is a cyclical model‚Äîmeaning post-purchase experience influences future decisions.

üîπ Process:
‚úî The company sends out a message (advertising, promotions).
‚úî The consumer receives the message and interprets it based on their experiences, beliefs, and personality.

üí° Example:
	‚Ä¢	Nike launches an ad campaign about its new running shoes highlighting comfort and technology.

‚úî The consumer compares different brands.
‚úî They evaluate price, features, brand reputation, and peer influence.
‚úî If the consumer finds the product suitable and beneficial, they move to the next step.

üí° Example:
	‚Ä¢	The consumer compares Nike‚Äôs shoes with Adidas.
	‚Ä¢	They check online reviews, store recommendations, and social media influencers.
	‚Ä¢	If the evaluation is positive, they decide to buy Nike shoes.

3Ô∏è‚É£ Field 3: Purchase Decision

‚úî The consumer selects the brand and buys the product.
‚úî The decision is influenced by availability, discounts, brand loyalty, and convenience.

4Ô∏è‚É£ Field 4: Post-Purchase Behavior & Feedback Loop

üîπ Possible Outcomes:
‚úî Positive Experience ‚Üí Satisfaction ‚Üí Brand Loyalty.
